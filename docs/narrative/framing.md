# Framing lines (verbatim)

> The canonical open and close copy for the demo (WS-7). These are the words said
> on screen and out loud. The opening line is enforced in code by
> `frontend/src/components/library/NarrativeFrame.tsx` (`OPENING_FRAMING_LINE`).
> Honesty guardrail 5: the opening framing line must be present at open.

## Opening (verbatim — do not alter)

> The engine is real. The data is representative, shaped like yours. Wiring it to your Yardi is what the first phase does.

This is the honesty contract stated up front. It sets three expectations before a
single number appears: the engine is the real Hauska/Mox stack; the data on screen
is representative and shaped like the prospect's portfolio (not their live book);
and connecting it to their actual Yardi is the work of the first engagement phase,
not something already done. Everything after this line is read against it.

## Transition (operating layer → the deal)

> That is the everyday operating layer. Now let me show you a real Austin redevelopment running through the same system, the kind of deal your Invest team underwrites.

One narrative, two data contexts. The transition exists so the operating beats and
the deal beats read as one operating system rather than two demos.

## Closing (polished demo copy)

> Everything you just saw runs on a spine you own. The interface you have been
> using is adaptive — it assembled each view around the question you asked, and as
> it does that it quietly absorbs the jobs of the point tools you pay for today.
> The dashboard, the variance report, the static investor PDF: those are features
> of the surface now, not separate line items. Over time the tools whose jobs it
> replaces simply go away.
>
> And it is cheaper for a structural reason, not a discount. We retrieve at the
> level of the atom — the single fact with its source, its reasoning, and its
> confidence — not by re-reading whole documents every time you ask. You pay to
> touch the fact you need, not the haystack around it. Atom-first retrieval is why
> the economics work.
>
> Now the part nobody says out loud. To get your own data out of Yardi today — a
> clean, structured interface to the system of record you already pay for — costs
> about twenty-five thousand dollars per interface per year, and you need Yardi's
> permission to build it. Your own data, rented back to you, gated by your vendor.
> On the spine, your data is yours. It carries its provenance, it travels with the
> asset, and it gets sharper every time your team touches it. That is the
> sovereignty point, and it is the whole reason to own the spine instead of renting
> the dashboard.

### Honesty notes on the close (for the presenter)

- The $25k-per-interface-per-year figure is the grounded sovereignty point: it is
  the real cost of a licensed Yardi interface, with Yardi's permission. State it as
  the cost of getting your own data *out*, not as our pricing.
- "Absorbs the tools whose jobs it replaces" is a statement about the adaptive
  interface, said in present/over-time tense. It is not a claim that any tool has
  already been decommissioned in this demo.
- The close must not introduce any live-bidirectional-Yardi claim (guardrail 6).
  The spine reads + assists + captures; write-back is a roadmap item gated on a
  licensed interface.
